Description

舍利子。色不異空。空不異色。色即是空。空即是色。
This verse is intended to intoduce the non-dual worldview that is key to enlightenment in Mahayana Buddhism. For ordinary people, this attempt at non-dualism is quickly broken. How could you say that emptiness, like space, is form? How could you say that form, like your body, is empty?

What if you consider form and void as interdependent, like yin and yang? One does not obtain meaning without the other; one cannot exist without the other. Perhaps this interpretation is still too simplistic. Is it a limitation of the English language that we cannot conceive of a non-dual world, of no birth, no death, no old age, no wisdom, nothing to attain?

But it is not just English that has yet come to terms with non-duality. Confucius texts like "The Great Learning" also encourage a dualistic perspective. Consider the last line of the Great Learning:
其所厚者薄、而其所薄者厚、未之有也。
It never has been the case that what was of great importance has been slightly cared for, and, at the same time, that what was of slight importance has been greatly cared for.
You simply cannot take the essential things as superficial, and the superficial things as essential.
I have provided two English translations of the Chinese. The second translation has stronger phrasing than the first.

Consider another passage in the Great Learning:
物有本末、事有終始。知所先後則近道矣。
Things have their root and their branches. Affairs have their end and their beginning. To know what is first and what is last will lead near to what is taught in the Great Learning.
Things have their roots and branches, affairs have their end and beginning. When you know what comes first and what comes last, then you are near the Way.
Perhaps those who are familiar with the Great Learning will argue that its objective is distinct; it is a political text. You may say thta Buddhism is a religion which chooses to not involve itself in affiars of human governance. It is dualistically separate, concerned with people's salvation and spiritual wholeness. If that is so, what does the Great Learning mean by 修身 and 德, to cultivate self and virtue? What kind of self cultivation does the investigation of things and extension of knowledge and sincerity of will and correction of mind lead to? Is it primarily an intellectual extension, a strong adherence to Confucians ideals of ritual and propriety? Or will a ruler who chooses to pursue the path prescribed by the Great Learning follow a path more similar to that of the Inward Training (內業) and cultivate self through meditation and Qi control?

One way to break free of the idea of roots and branches is to consider that the stream of time is a Mobius strip. The Mobius strip is so long that we can only see a small, linear section of it. Our limited perception gives us reason to believe in beginning and end, birth and death, as ultimate phenomenon. If the Mobius strip interpretation is accurate, then birth and death, us, even, are merely being shuttled endlessly along this stream of life. Is this Samsara?

Even if you are able to recognize Samsara, you have not begun your journey towards Nirvana and Prajna Paramita until you recognize non-duality. Ackonwledgement of non-duality can come simply if you expand the scale at which you consider the universe. If you consider the universe to encompass all of us and the things on this earth and the galaxy, etc, then there is only unity. Duality only appears when you zoom in and borders between objects begin appearing.

The above analysis may be too superficial, but is only the first step to breaking free of samsara. As you proceed through the Heart Sutra and into deeper levels of understanding, you will likely discover a better way to demonstrate the emptiness of form and 'formness' of the Void.
